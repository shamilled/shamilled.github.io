When a baby leopard is very small, its skin pattern (also known as animal print) starts to develop. Its body is big so spots form, but the tail is long and skinny so it becomes striped. It's all because of math and the spreading of melanin and its inhibitor.

How do leopards get spots?
Imagine a race! At a certain time a flag is dropped and the people start running. Some people are slower at running than others. Well here, the slow runners are the melanin and the fast runners are the inhibitors! At a certain time skin cells start making melanin, which makes them colored. However, the same cells are making an inhibitor that is telling the surrounding skin cells NOT to make melanin, which means they are a natural color.

Why is a leopard's tail striped?
MATH! When the stripes on a leopard's tail were being developed the tail was long and thin like a pencil. When the melanin and inhibitors started spreading, they only had a little space to grow and stripes formed! The stripes on a leopard's tail are there because of math!
